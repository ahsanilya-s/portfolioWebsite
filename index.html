About

Operations first, software second

Most software fails in NGOs for the same reason: it was designed around technology instead of the people and workflows it has to serve. I work the other way around — I sit inside an international NGO, so I see how reporting deadlines, field constraints, and donor requirements actually play out before I write a line of code.

That means I already understand the things an outside developer has to be taught: why a form must work with no connectivity, why supervisors need a documented trail when they return a submission, why ActivityInfo sync matters for donor reporting, and why the field team's reality — not the office's — decides whether a system gets used at all.

I own an international NGO's Pakistan-mission systems end-to-end: architecture, build, deployment, and the long tail of maintenance. B.S. Software Engineering, Air University (2026). Based in Islamabad — available for remote engagements with NGOs, INGOs, and donor-funded projects.

Built around field reality

Tools designed for how field teams actually work — no connectivity, shared devices, tight monitoring visits — not how an office assumes they work.

Reporting that holds up

Data pipelines built to survive donor scrutiny — one source of truth from field submission to ActivityInfo to the report a donor actually reads.

Delivered, then supported

Programme deadlines are real deadlines. I ship on them — then document, monitor, and support the system long after launch, because handover without support is how tools die.

CD-CRI SHA Toolkit

The challenge — building-safety screening ran on paper checklists and scattered spreadsheets: slow to consolidate, hard to verify, and painful to report against. Engineers and custodians needed one structured way to assess buildings and see results roll up.

  • Who uses it — PEC-accredited engineers run the full technical RVS; facility custodians run a simplified pre-screen. Two workflows, one platform.
  • What changed — assessments across 6 hazard modules now arrive structured, with photo evidence and recommendation codes, instead of being compiled by hand.
  • Reporting — supervisors watch a live roll-up dashboard, and results sync to ActivityInfo so M&E and donor reporting draw from one source.

UN-Habitat TPM Field App

The challenge — third-party monitoring happens where connectivity fails. Paper forms and after-the-fact data entry meant transcription errors, lost time, and no clear trail between what a monitor saw and what a supervisor approved.

  • Who uses it — field monitors and supervisors on UN-Habitat third-party monitoring assignments across Pakistan.
  • What changed — monitors download form packs, work fully offline, and sync safely when back in coverage; supervisors return submissions for fixes with a documented trail instead of phone calls and re-typed forms.
  • Accountability — a GRM / FCRM complaints module and a bilingual English–Urdu UI, built for the staff who actually hold the device.

JavaSpector

The challenge — reviewing Java projects for code quality was manual, slow, and inconsistent — every reviewer applied different standards, and feedback arrived too late to act on.

  • Who uses it — adopted by Air University's Software Engineering department as a reference tool for reviewing student codebases.

  • Visibility — visual architecture reports and grading replace ad-hoc written feedback.

FleetSync

The challenge — vehicle booking and fleet lifecycle tracking is the kind of operational workflow that usually lives in spreadsheets and phone calls — no audit trail, no role separation, no single view of the fleet.

  • Why it was built — to give operations staff one place to book vehicles, manage users, and track fleet lifecycle, with roles enforced instead of assumed.
  • What changed — booking, user management, and lifecycle each became an auditable service with JWT / RBAC access control — who did what is always answerable.
  • Delivery — led a team of 3 from design to AWS deployment with MySQL persistence.
